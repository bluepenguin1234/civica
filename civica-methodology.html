How the Civica Score is built

54 data points. 8 pillars. One composite score. Here's exactly what we measure, how we weight it, and where the data comes from.

Civica Score (0–100)

Each pillar is scored 0–100 based on available data. Pillar scores are multiplied by their weight and summed to produce the composite Civica Score. When data is missing, pillar scores are estimated conservatively and the town's confidence rating is adjusted accordingly. The composite score is authoritative — individual pillar estimates are directional.

Tax Efficiency Ratio (TER) — Civica Proprietary Metric

The TER measures what residents get for what they pay. A town can have a high tax bill but deliver exceptional schools, safety, and infrastructure — making it high value. Conversely, a low-tax town with failing schools and poor services scores poorly on TER.

TER = (School Score × 0.40) + (Safety Score × 0.25) + (Infrastructure Score × 0.20) + (Fiscal Score × 0.15) ÷ Normalized Tax Burden
Pillar Breakdown
🏦

1. Fiscal Health

Is the town financially sound?

30% of score ▼

Fiscal health is the most heavily weighted pillar because a town's finances determine its ability to maintain services, avoid tax hikes, and fund schools over the long term. A fiscally distressed municipality can collapse services quickly — and home values with them. We weight bond rating and free cash most heavily because they are the most independently verified and forward-looking indicators.

Data points measured
S&P Bond Rating
Free Cash % of Budget
Pension Funded Ratio
Debt Per Capita
GFOA Certificate (consecutive yrs)
Financial Transparency (ACFR)
Sources: S&P Global Ratings, MA DLS, EMMA (MSRB), town ACFRs, GFOA.org
💰

2. Tax Efficiency

What's the real cost of living here?

15% of score ▼

We look beyond the sticker price of a tax bill to understand the effective burden on residents. Municipal electric utilities can save households $1,500–$2,000/year — a benefit invisible to buyers using Zillow. The non-residential tax base share matters because towns with strong commercial bases subsidize residential rates.

Data points measured
Effective Tax Rate
Median Annual Tax Bill
Median Household Income
Residential Rate (per $1,000 AV)
Non-Residential Tax Base %
Tax Efficiency Ratio (TER)
Sources: MA DLS (Division of Local Services), Census ACS, town ACFRs, MMLD/PMLP/Danvers Electric annual reports
🎓

3. Schools

Quality and trajectory of public education

15% of score ▼

School quality is a primary driver of home values and quality of life for families. We use statewide rank (not raw scores) so towns are comparable regardless of size. Critically, we also track the 10-year rank change — a school trending upward is a leading indicator of neighborhood quality improvement; one falling signals something important.

Data points measured
State District Rank (of 351)
10-Year Rank Change
MCAS Math Proficiency %
4-Year Graduation Rate
AP Participation Rate
Per-Pupil Spending
Sources: MA DESE (Dept. of Elementary & Secondary Education), SchoolDigger, MA Report Cards
⚙️

4. Operational Responsiveness

How well does the town actually function day-to-day?

10% of score ▼

This pillar measures execution quality — how fast potholes get fixed, how quickly EMS arrives, how easy it is to get a building permit. Many towns in this cohort have provisional scores for this pillar due to limited publicly available 311 and EMS response data. We flag this transparently in each profile.

Data points measured
311 Response Time (avg days)
EMS Response Time (avg min)
Building Permits per 1,000 Residents
ISO Fire Rating
ACFR Publication (financial transparency)
Sources: Town open data portals, ISO (Insurance Services Office), MA 911 reporting, GFOA
⚡

5. Infrastructure & Utilities

What does the town give you to get around and stay connected?

10% of score ▼

Municipal electric utilities create a permanent, recurring savings advantage over Eversource — we quantify this in dollars per year so buyers can include it in their total cost of ownership calculation. Commuter rail access has an outsized impact on property values and quality of life for Boston-area workers.

Data points measured
Electric Savings vs State Avg ($/yr)
Water Quality Violations (5yr)
Broadband Coverage %
Transit Access (rail/bus/none)
Walk Score
Sources: Muni utility annual reports, EPA ECHO, FCC broadband data, MBTA, Walk Score API
🛡️

6. Safety & Amenities

Crime rates, parks, and public library access

5% of score ▼

We use per-capita crime rates (per 100,000 residents) for comparability across towns of different sizes. Violent crime receives higher weight than property crime within this pillar. Park access and library circulation are included as quality-of-life indicators.

Data points measured
Violent Crime per 100k
Property Crime per 100k
Crime 5-Year Trend (%)
Park Acres per 1,000 Residents
Library Circulations per Capita
Sources: FBI Crime Data Explorer (CDE), MA State Police, Trust for Public Land ParkScore, MBLC (MA Board of Library Commissioners)
📈

7. Economic Momentum

Is the town growing stronger or stagnating?

10% of score ▼

A town's economic trajectory predicts its future quality of services, tax base stability, and home value appreciation. Income growth and educational attainment are the strongest leading indicators we've found for long-term civic health.

Data points measured
Median Income Growth (10yr)
Population Growth (10yr)
Bachelor's Degree or Higher %
Unemployment Rate
Poverty Rate
Sources: US Census Bureau ACS (American Community Survey), Bureau of Labor Statistics
🌿

8. Climate Resilience

How exposed is the town to long-term climate risk?

5% of score ▼

Climate risk is increasingly priced into home values and insurance premiums. We look at both current flood exposure and the 30-year trajectory — a coastal town with rising flood risk may face a very different insurance market by 2050 than today's premiums suggest.

Data points measured
Flood Risk — Current Properties %
Flood Risk Growth by 2050 (pts)
Wildfire Risk Rating
Additional Heat Days by 2050
Air Quality AQI (annual avg)
Tree Canopy Coverage %
Sources: First Street Foundation, FEMA NFIP, EPA AirNow, Climate Central, USDA Forest Service

Our editorial principles

  • We cite every data point. If a source isn't verifiable, we mark the field as pending rather than estimate.
  • We flag data gaps explicitly and adjust the confidence rating. A score is only as trustworthy as the data behind it.
  • We do not accept payment from towns, real estate agencies, or developers to adjust scores.
  • Scores are updated when material new data is published — not on a fixed calendar. We note the data vintage on each profile.
  • The Honest Buyer Take is written from the buyer's perspective, not the town's. We include red flags even for high-scoring towns.
  • When a bond rating changes due to a methodology shift rather than the town's fundamentals, we note it in the compiler notes.

Data confidence ratings

High All major fiscal, school, and safety data points are sourced from primary records. Score is stable.
Medium A few fields estimated or sourced from secondary data. Score is directionally reliable but may shift by ±3–5 points.
Low Multiple key fields are pending primary source verification. Score should be treated as preliminary. Do not rely on it alone for a purchase decision.
